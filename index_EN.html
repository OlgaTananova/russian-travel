Travelling across Russia

Have you ever wondered what lies beyond the news headlines about Russia?

What haven't we seen there?

According to VTsIOM polls, while 95% of Russians dream of traveling abroad, only 36% plan to spend their vacation in their own country. Yet, Russia is a diverse and breathtaking universe, encompassing the gentle seas of the south, the dense forests of Sayan, and the harsh ice of the Putorana Plateau. The best part? You don't need to have millions in your bank account, a passport, or endure long flights to experience these natural wonders. Take inspiration from Vera Bashmakova, a brave young mother who took her three children on a 20,000-kilometer road trip across her native country in her trusty Lada car.

  • The Curonian Spit

    URL

    park-kosa.ru

    The Curonian Spit is a stunning natural area located in the Kaliningrad Oblast, where you can witness two contrasting water horizons. To one side lies the peaceful Curonian Lagoon, while on the other, the Baltic Sea ripples with waves. The unique landscape of forests and sandy dunes makes it a must-visit destination in Russia.

    But the Kaliningrad Oblast has more to offer. Nearby is the westernmost point of Russia - the Baltic Spit - and a collection of small coastal towns that reflect their German heritage. The atmosphere of these places is calm and peaceful, perfect for visitors seeking a break from the hustle and bustle of city life. Immersing yourself in the tranquility of nature and breathing in the refreshing scent of the cool sea breeze is an experience you won't forget.

  • Kola Peninsula

    URL

    National Geographic

    The majority of the peninsula lies beyond the Arctic Circle, with the Sami tundra to the north, the taiga to the south, and the Arctic Ocean, masquerading as the Barents Sea, to the north.

    Perhaps you have seen Zvyagintsev's film or heard of the Arctic festival in Teriberka. Maybe the word "Khibiny" is not buried under the snowdrift of your school memories of geography lessons. Perhaps you haven't been interested in the super-deep borehole that penetrates the earth's crust, or the apatite deposits no longer spark your curiosity. However, your dream of seeing the Northern Lights begins to come true with a ticket to Murmansk.

  • Altai

    URL

    Facebook

    Altai is undoubtedly one of the most beautiful places in Russia, thanks to its majestic mountains, lush pine forests, pristine rivers and lakes. As you travel along the Altai mountain range, you'll be greeted by breathtaking views of towering peaks and winding valleys, with fragrant pine trees dotting the landscape. Opening the car windows, you'll be hit by the invigorating mountain air, a true wonder of this region.

    The Altai climate is moderate, making summer the best time to visit if you want to experience the full range of local flora and fauna. Here, you'll encounter a wide variety of wildlife, such as elk roaming through the forests, eagles soaring above the peaks, and deer grazing on the plains. You might even catch a glimpse of the famous manuls, who call the Altai region their home.

  • Winter at Lake Baikal

    URL

    https://vk.com/baikalmile

    Winter at Lake Baikal is truly a sight to behold. Known as the largest freshwater lake in the world, Baikal is also one of the most stunning destinations in Russia. But there's more to Baikal than meets the eye. It's also an ideal location for ski-joring competitions.

    For those unfamiliar with the sport, ski-joring involves a skier being tethered to a motorbike, with the pair aiming to reach high speeds across the ice. In March 2019, the "Baikal Mile" festival set a new world record for ski-joring, with a top speed of 197.011 km/h. This festival is just one example of the many unique events and activities that take place at Lake Baikal, making it an unforgettable destination for visitors from around the globe.

  • Karelia

    URL

    http://vodlozero.ru

    Siberia is often thought to end at the Ural Mountains, but in reality, it extends into Karelia. The Siberian larch, which forms the taiga, does not grow west of Lake Vodlozero, but in Karelian national parks, it reaches heights of up to 30 meters. These forests have never known the sound of an ax due to the impenetrable swamps that surround them. Some of the pines here are over five hundred years old, having witnessed the sun long before Ivan the Terrible did. You won't find a single trail in the virgin forest for hundreds of kilometers. And on the rare paths that do exist, trees a few meters off the ground are marked with bear claws to let everyone know who is in charge here.
